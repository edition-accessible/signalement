High impact informations for readers with specific needs

Talk given at the Library of Congress for IFLA Libraries Serving Persons with Print Disabilities Seminar in Washington, D.C. August 2024.

Reading a publication is a deeply personal experience. We all have our routines-a trip to the bookstore, a search for our favorite title, or a casual browse through the best-seller list. Then, we purchase and dive into the publication. It's a simple, yet profound act.

Consider being visually impaired and relying on assistive technology to read a publication. You may wonder: Will my screen reader be compatible with this title? Are there image descriptions available for the visuals? Are there accessible page numbers? Is the reading order correct to avoid potential hazards? These are just a few of the accessibility concerns for consumers when buying and reading a digital publication.

For years, because ebooks are not available in the standard distribution chain (bookstores or libraries) and because information about accessibility features of ebooks is not available, readers with specific needs have relied on Library Service to Persons with Disabilities, which provides adaptations of books in specialised formats, with all known limitations.

But the landscape is changing. More and more publishers are now creating publications that are Born Accessible. These are not just accessible from the outset, but also come with detailed information about their accessibility features. It's a promising shift in the industry.

However, the success of these efforts hinges on one crucial factor: the end users. Without their understanding and engagement, all our work would be in vain. They are not just readers, but an integral part of the ecosystem. It’s imperative to involve them in our projects and ensure we serve them as best as possible.

That’s why I want to discuss with you today high-impact information for readers with specific needs, how to get it, how to display it, and how to understand it.

Allow me to introduce myself. I'm Gautier Chomel, a veteran in book production with a quarter century of experience. Half of my career has been dedicated to making books available for people with print reading impairment. I've worked with esteemed organizations like the French Federation for the Blind, the Daisy Consortium, and the accessible book consortium. I now serve as a project manager for the European Digital Reading Lab (EDRLab), a professional membership association dedicated to open-source software and open standards for the publishing sector worldwide. Our mission is to deploy an open, interoperable, and accessible digital publishing ecosystem worldwide.

I have this crucial topic in my program portfolio to foster the best possible future accessible landscape. I’m happy to present that work on How Accessibility Metadata in a User-First Approach Creates a Seamless Experience for All. I hope it will inspire you to reproduce similar projects and implement High impact information for readers with specific needs in your catalogues and reading systems.

Before anything, we have to think about what constitutes an accessible ebook. We, experts, often say that an ebook is WCAG AA compliant, but WCAG is not accessibility; it is only a means of assessing it. I invite you to take a user-centric approach and always get back to the core question: How do people read?

In that approach, an accessible book is not the same for everyone, and we can need different ways to read depending on the time of the day, our cognitive load and many other factors.

  • Some people rely on visual reading but need to adjust text display and modify layout. This means we need to be able to change fonts, sizes, spaces, margins, justification, color, etc., and as many personalisation options as possible to help us access reading visually.
  • We can need a complementary read-aloud of the texts. For that experience to be agreeable, we rely on voice selection, spoken text highlights, and rate and pitch options. We must also be able to set up and fine-tune captions, suitability, clean view, and split text, among other possibilities.
  • Those who rely on assistive technologies like screen readers to read through braille or audio outputs with rich structure and interactions need robust information like roles and states but can also set up levels of verbosity and feedback.

An accessible book is one that meets readers' needs, and not all readers have the exact same needs. Therefore, accessibility is not a binary yes or no. Users need as much information as possible to decide if a book is accessible to them.

It’s a paradigm shift from a format-based approach: accessible ebooks such as Word, DAISY, and EPUB to a Feature-based perspective: ebooks allow Visual Adjustments, visual Reading, and rich Navigation…

The format approach does not say if images are described in text or if print pagination is available. PDFs are known to be poorly accessible, but they can still be readable by screen readers if they have been produced properly. On the other hand, most daisy audio-only volunteer records have very partial accessibility features outside of being human narrated, precisely because of the time-consuming non-professional production.

The challenge now is to know what features an ebook includes, independently of the format. This applies to trade ebooks as well as to specialised libraries or NGO productions.

With that reader-first approach, we don’t know how many ebooks are accessible. The blocker here is the format-defined collection approach. We know there are 1 million titles available in ABC global book service, 180 thousand titles are available in braille and audio in the NLS catalogue, and 73 thousand Daisy audio are available in the French Association Valentin Hauy specialised collections.

That does not tell us how many ebooks are readable with visual adjustments or how many have print pagination navigation.

Those of us who produce and manage those collections know that they are largely human-narrated books with no text synchronisation, few navigation options, and fewer advanced features.

On the trade side, we know that many commercial ebooks are already used for reading by persons with print disabilities, but the choice is a bit of a gambling.

The Canadian-based distributor De Marque presented in October 2023 in Frankfurt an analysis of the 2.2 million epub files available in his catalogues, including the entire catalogue of the Big 5 publishers in the US.

Only 3% of ebooks have any accessibility information available in ONIX (so for retailer use), while close to 10% had this information available in the file. A simple file analysis proved that 93% of them had at least one accessibility feature.

That could be a non relevant information, so let’s be more precise. From the same work, looking at publications entirely textual (so readable with a screen reader), no information available in ONIX, 5% of titles with that information in the file and 15% inferred from simple automated analysis. That’s 300 thousand titles that are readable today for a blind person. We just don’t know it. That’s a very minimum, there’s probably more than double if we succeed in isolating decorative images!

So many accessible ebooks are invisible. Thanks to inference and file analysis, we have ways to fix that, and we know that more and more publishers are looking at understanding the accessibility of their collections. Expect huge and quick changes in the near future.

That’s not sufficient since this information is not displayed and provided in a coherent way, everywhere.

There have been pioneers in displaying accessibility features, such as Bookshare, De Marque, Epagine, Libri Italiani Accessibili, and NNELS, and I’m certainly missing others.

They have done great work, but since we are not working together and agreeing on common ways, that leads to a random user experience. No one wants to reproduce the electric plug's international failure. Visual guides to travellers' needs make a funny smiley collection but what a mess!

That’s why the Accessibility task force of publishing @w3C group worked on international guidance on how to display accessibility information to end users. This work lead by usual suspects, such as Daisy, Benetech, Fondazione LIA and EDRLab issued a first version in 2021 and a soon-to-be-published second version much better thanks to feedback received.

It is a set of documents that provide an interpretation framework for machine-readable metadata, until now including ONIX and EPUB metadata, we hope soon more set, especially from the MARC landscape as it is the standard for libraries.

How does that work? I’m describing the schema: Digital Publication Ecosystem flows into four areas: Metadata Delivery, Accessibility Metadata spec, Metadata Processing, and Resulting Statements in two ways: from embedded into EPUB to supply chain annexe information (ONIX). Both are to get the same user experience.

Let’s make it less abstractive :

With the appropriate metadata, a publisher claims that an ebook has

  • accessibilityFeature displayTransformability.
  • accessModeSufficient textual
  • tableOfContents, pageNavigation
  • chartOnVisual, longDescriptions

The reader looking at the book information page gets the following information:

  • Appearance can be modified.
  • Readable in read aloud and braille.
  • Navigation by table of contents and page list.
  • Charts and diagrams have extended descriptions.

The User Experience Guide for Displaying Accessibility Metadata 2.0 is actually a Draft Community Group Report, the address to access is available in the accessible version of that presentation. We are still looking for Feedback through our GitHub issue tracker.

It states Key information and example wordings for the most important accessibility claims like Visual Adjustment, Non-visual reading, Conformance, Pre-recorded audio, Navigation, Charts diagrams, formulas and hazards as well as guidance for additional accessibility information like Braille availability, clean audio, dyslexia readability and others. For each, a descriptive and a compact example are proposed to help each distributor to fit its reality (available place on the page, main public focus, etc.). Search filters are also part of the guidance.

The onscreen example is about Navigation, we can read:

  • Example 11: Descriptive explanations
    • Contains a table of contents that provides direct access to all chapters of the text via links.
    • Index provides links to item references.
    • Landmarks provide quick access to main parts of the book.
    • A page list enables users to coordinate their reading with a statically paginated version.
  • Example 12: Compact explanation
    • Navigation by table of contents, index, landmarks, and page list.

How do we work to get Signposting accessibility of ebooks doable with the industry tools but also as relevant as possible for readers. In other words, how do we make sure our guide is as user-centric as possible?

This has been done thru localized projects considering readers as part of the ecosystem.

At W3C we made an important part of our feedback collection with National working groups and companies to present their work and participate. This also implied for us to Follow up on national projects to guarantee homogeneity of data collected.

The diagram shows how recommendations were tested in France with the help of EDRLab, in Italy by the LIA Foundation, In the Netherlands by Dedicon, in Canada by De Marque and les Librarire.ca (this list is not exhaustive) and how each project feedback nourished the rewriting of the guide.

I’ll detail the example I know best, the French implementation project that we called Signposting accessibility of ebooks.

It started in 2020 with a landscape analysis, then a set of prototypes based on the first W3C recommendation. Those prototypes where submitted to 54 persons from 30 organisations including associations representing readers with special needs. Two questions where asked: is the user experience understandable and is it doable? Responses were almost negative, so we took action to enhance the availability of metadata, and the expressable options in different metadata standards and built new prototypes to iterate on other workshops. For the wording, we built a localized vocabulary study including different users to take part in to open survey as well as controlled panels. Once we had reached a satisfactory point, we moved on from the prototypes to the validation of the concepts by the bookseller portal suppliers. They carried out individual usability tests and gathered extensive feedback.

With the soon-to-be-released next version of the display guide, we’ll encourage as many implementations as possible and continue to collect feedback.

This led us to build aside tools like a dictionary of accessibility metadata (in French) for who has to set up this information and a use case functionality list (in French).

The project methodology and the tools we’ve built for it are available as open source, and we welcome anyone who wishes to use them for a similar project in their country. In addition to the methodology itself, it includes References, prototypes, vocabulary study, reading use cases, and a publisher-friendly metadata dictionary.

You can find everything at the Signposting accessible digital books project webpage. There’s an explicative section in English, but most of the material is still in French. We’ll be happy to help with translations, project set-up, monitoring, communication, and international recognition.

That’s important for the ecosystem, it’s about understanding and appropriation, so we invite you to make your own localised project.

So please re-use anything you need: Methodology, references, prototypes, vocabulary study, reading use cases, and a publisher-friendly metadata dictionary. Improve the tools and make them yours. We help with translations, project set-up, monitoring, communication, and international recognition.

You can contact me for anything.

I would like to end with a view to the roadmap, which outlines what we see as necessary to keep building a better future for people's access to reading.

Publishing is no longer a top-down activity; there are no longer knowledge masters in ivory towers. With digital, interactions in the value chain become not only possible but also necessary. The reader can change the Layout and even switch between the senses used for reading.

A born accessible future is made of shared responsibilities between publishers, distributors, reading systems and readers.

For that to work in a rich landscape of diverse players, vendor-neutral solutions built by strong community-driven organisations at the local and international levels are needed.

  • There is is a need for more metadata—that was my introduction to this talk. We have ways to get quick changes thanks to inference rules, and there’s an open-source tool for that. It is the Radium Go toolkits that will help you with file analysis.
  • We need better information provided to end users. The display guide is here, and we urge you to implement it in your library catalogue.
  • It is necessary to foster readers' appropriation by associating them with proximity-understandable projects. Build your national project, reuse our materials, or invent yours. Don’t forget to keep us informed so your efforts can be valorized at the international level.
  • Stronger interoperability between specialised and general reading systems is a building camp to make sure that we get Format agnostic reading apps & devices
